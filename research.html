Research

Research Statement

My research is centered at the intersection of Machine Learning, Signal Processing, and Computer Vision, with a primary focus on developing robust, multi-modal intelligent systems. I am driven by the challenge of fusing information from disparate data sources to build models that are more accurate, generalizable, and interpretable than their unimodal counterparts.

A significant portion of my work is dedicated to applications in the biomedical domain, including diagnostic imaging, bioinformatics, and the analysis of physiological signals. I am passionate about creating technologies that can lead to tangible improvements in healthcare. Concurrently, I explore the application of these computational methods to analyze complex socio-political systems, aiming to uncover data-driven insights into human behavior. My ultimate goal is to advance the frontier of multi-modal AI and apply it to solve meaningful, real-world problems through collaborative and open research.

Thesis Projects

An Extensive Study on D2C: Overfitting Remediation in Deep Learning Using a Decentralized Approach

M.Sc. in Computer Science and Engineering

This thesis investigated a novel decentralized framework to mitigate overfitting in deep learning. By training simpler models on disjoint data subsets and employing an uncertainty-aware selection mechanism, the study demonstrates significant improvements in generalization, especially in data-heterogeneous environments.

Supervisor: Prof. Dr. Golam Rabiul Alam

Thesis Link Preprint GitHub

A DUAL-DOMAIN APPROACH TO MEDICAL IMAGE SEGMENTATION & CLASSIFICATION BASED ON SPATIAL AND SPECTRAL SUMMARIZER NETWORK FUSION

M.Sc. in Electrical and Electronic Engineering

This research introduces S³F-Net, a novel multi-modal architecture that leverages Vision Transformers to fuse spatial and spectral information from medical images, leading to more accurate and robust classification and segmentation of anomalies like brain tumors.

Supervisor: Prof. Dr. Mohammed Imamul Hassan Bhuiyan

Preprint GitHub

Bioradiolocation-Based Multi-Class Sleep Stage Classification

B.Sc. in Electrical and Electronic Engineering

The undergraduate thesis focused on a non-invasive sleep stage classification method using bioradiolocation signals. By extracting time-frequency features and employing a Random Forest classifier, the system achieved high accuracy comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

Supervisor: Prof. Dr. Mohammed Imamul Hassan Bhuiyan

Ongoing Research Projects

Repression and Mobilization Analysis in Bangladesh

A collaborative project with a Ph.D. student from the University of Oxford, this research applies ML and statistical modeling to analyze the dynamics of state repression and citizen mobilization during Bangladesh's July Revolution. One paper from this work is already under review.

Multi-Modal Fusion for Biomedical Audio Analysis (AudioFuse)

As part of our BIOML (Bioinformatics, Imaging & Omics using Machine Learning) lab, this project explores advanced fusion techniques for biomedical audio signals. The research aims to combine different signal representations, including waveforms, spectrograms, and scalograms, to build more robust diagnostic models. One paper from this research is currently under review.

Advanced Fusion Methods for Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) Prediction

Also part of the BIOML lab, this research builds upon our accepted paper on AMR prediction. The current focus is on investigating sophisticated feature fusion approaches to improve predictive accuracy, moving beyond the soft-voting ensemble methods used previously.
